Cognitive Archetypes — Four Ways of Being Stuck

Each participant arrived with a different "cognitive operating system." The same classroom, the same AI tool, and the same discussion format produced four entirely different experiences of friction and adaptation.

Intuition-Driven
The Reluctant Performer
Former journalist · Neurodivergent (self-identified) · High social anxiety · "Small-street-smart" learning style
"I just don't want to discuss. The questions for discussion are worthless. Better to spend that time doing something real."
Classroom discussion feels like forced theatrical performance, not learning
Goal-Oriented
The Structured Adapter
Former teacher · ChatGPT power-user · Extroverted but anxious · Views exchange as transaction
"Talking to others is just exchanging viewpoints. I don't see why it has to be so emotional."
AI becomes a "cognitive prosthetic" for tasks that feel socially inefficient
System-Seeking
The Collapsing Optimizer
Former philosophy student · High self-efficacy · First-semester breakdown · P-to-J transition under pressure
"I thought I could handle it. Then the first semester hit, and I realized my entire system didn't work here."
Productivity systems imported from home culture collapse under new institutional logic
Multi-Layered
The Gradual Transformer
Long-term observer · ChatGPT dependent · Three-year trajectory visible · Multiple diagnoses
"Looking back, I can't tell whether I adapted to the system or the system finally gave up on changing me."
Long-term adaptation may be camouflaged resignation, not genuine integration

Emotional Arc — From Honeymoon to Double Bind

Semester-by-Semester Emotional Trajectory
Month 1-3
Excited, overwhelmed, "everything is possible"
Month 4-6
Confused, self-blame, "why can't I keep up?"
Month 7-12
Numb, cynical, "this is just how it is"
Year 2
Strategic, selective, "I take what I need"
Graduation
Ambivalent pride, unresolved grief
Key insight: The emotional curve does not follow a simple "culture shock" U-shape. It plateaus in a double bind—participants feel they must perform a version of themselves they do not believe in, while abandoning skills they once trusted. Products designed for "international students" often target the honeymoon phase and miss the long-tail crisis.

Core Findings — What the Data Revealed

01

The Parallel-Classroom Effect

Group discussions were described by multiple participants as "two parallel worlds separated by glass." Chinese students and local students operated in the same physical space but with incompatible interaction scripts.
"Every time we've had a group discussion, it feels like we're two parallel worlds separated by glass." — Social media post
→ Product implication: Collaboration features cannot assume shared cultural norms for "discussion." Real-time translation or shared documents do not bridge fundamentally different turn-taking, disagreement, and silence protocols.
02

ChatGPT as "Brainful yet Brainless"

Participants did not treat ChatGPT as a search engine or a cheat tool. They treated it as a social interlocutor—someone to bounce ideas off, someone who would not judge their "stupid questions," someone who could translate their fragmented thoughts into coherent prose.
"ChatGPT is the only one who understands what I am trying to say before I have finished saying it." — Interview
→ Product implication: The killer feature for cross-cultural users is not accuracy but interpretive generosity—the ability to reconstruct half-formed thoughts without shaming the user. Current AI products optimize for correctness; they should optimize for recognition.
03

The Workshop Portfolio Trap

A recurring assessment format—the iterative portfolio—was experienced not as "learning by doing" but as "performing confusion for a grade." Students felt forced to display a process they did not actually experience.
"The workshop is basically writing over and over with a half-baked framework, then realising the framework doesn't work—but for the sake of this course, I still have to cobble something together."
→ Product implication: Process-tracking features (version history, iteration logs) can become surveillance tools when the institutional culture values performative struggle over outcome. Users will game the system to produce the appearance of process.
04

Oracy vs. Literacy — The Hidden Interface War

Students trained in highly literate, exam-oriented systems experienced seminar-style oracy as a form of cognitive violence. Their strengths—structured writing, memorization, hierarchical argumentation—were devalued; their weaknesses—spontaneous speech, casual debate, performative confidence—were overvalued.
"I can write a perfect essay in Chinese. But here, if you cannot talk fast and loud in class, you are invisible."
→ Product implication: EdTech platforms that prioritize video responses, live chat, or verbal Q&A systematically disadvantage users from high-literacy cultures. Multimodal submission options (text, voice, visual) are not accessibility luxuries; they are equity requirements.
05

Neurodiversity as Cultural, Not Clinical

One participant with Asperger's and ADHD self-diagnosis navigated the system by finding a single "safe person" (the researcher) to relay all course information. When that channel closed, she adapted by extracting information from others' discussions and integrating it into her own work—without ever attending class.
"I stopped going to discussions. I just read what other people wrote and built my arguments from there. It was more reliable than the chaos of the room."
→ Product implication: "Inclusive design" often focuses on screen readers and color contrast. But for neurodivergent users in high-stakes social environments, the real need is asynchronous, structured information architectures that replace real-time social negotiation with predictable, navigable data flows.
06

Authorship Anxiety in the Age of AI

By the final semester, participants no longer asked "Is using ChatGPT cheating?" They asked "If ChatGPT wrote the sentence, but I agreed with it, is it still my thought?" The crisis shifted from external regulation to internal identity.
"I can't tell anymore whether I am thinking or just approving what the machine suggests."
→ Product implication: AI writing assistants need provenance features—not to detect plagiarism, but to help users reconstruct their own decision trail. "I chose this word because..." functions as an identity anchor in a sea of algorithmic suggestions.

Methodological Rigor — How the Study Was Built

🪞

Auto-ethnography

The researcher was simultaneously insider (Chinese international student) and outsider (anthropologist), using self-observation to calibrate interview questions and identify blind spots.

📱

Digital Behavior Tracking

Participants shared screenshots of ChatGPT conversations, learning-platform logs, and social-media posts, creating a longitudinal digital trace alongside verbal interviews.

🎭

Social Listening

Systematic collection of anonymous posts by Chinese international students on Xiaohongshu and WeChat, capturing emotions too raw to express in formal interviews.

🔄

Longitudinal Design

Same participants interviewed across four semesters, tracking how their relationship to tools, institutions, and their own identities transformed over time—not as a snapshot, but as a film.
