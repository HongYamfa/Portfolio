## Research

### (Re)Making Sense: Generative AI and the Cognitive Ecology of Transnational Students
**MSc Thesis · KU Leuven · Sep 2024 – Jul 2025**

A longitudinal ethnography of how Chinese international students enrol generative AI in their cognitive and affective adaptation to an unfamiliar academic culture. Across ten months of fieldwork with more than twenty-five interlocutors — combining dialogical interviews, participant observation, autoethnography, and the analysis of digital traces — the study attends to what conventional methods miss: silence, avoidance, and the unspoken textures of meaning-making.

Drawing on Gregory Bateson's ecology of mind and Tim Ingold's relational ontology, it maps three interlocking *double-binds* that surface when students lean on automated text systems: an **institutional** bind, between the exam-oriented habitus many arrive with and a seminar culture that rewards live, argumentative exchange; a **semiotic-algorithmic** bind, in which AI raises immediate linguistic fluency while seeding cognitive dependency, hallucination, and doubt about one's own reasoning; and an **affective-ethical** bind, between the relief AI offers and the unease of suspecting it erodes the very capacities one came abroad to build.

These binds are legible in how interlocutors actually speak about the technology. One described ChatGPT as resourceful yet "brainless" — indispensable for productivity, yet never a mind; another, averse to the whole culture of argument, refused it as a conversational companion and treated it strictly as an information-processing machine; a third found that carefully prepared comments still collapsed into silence in real-time seminar exchange. Read together, these are not idiosyncrasies but a vernacular epistemology that declines to equate linguistic output with understanding.

The thesis advances two conceptual contributions. The first is a distinction between *personality* and *personhood*: AI may accumulate a recognisable personality — a distinctive "colour," as one interlocutor put it — without thereby acquiring personhood, and conflating the two is precisely what generates the affective binds above. The second reframes the student not as an integrated individual undergoing "adaptation" but as a *dividual* who continually modulates heterogeneous resources — material, habitual, affective — to keep a sense of agency intact under conditions that are simultaneously institutional, semiotic, and emotional.

**Methods:** Dialogical interviews · Participant observation · Autoethnography · Digital trace analysis

### Digital Governance and Everyday Resistance: An Ethnography of the Health Code
**Independent Fieldwork · Nov 2021 – May 2022**

An ethnographic and computational study of how a single state-mandated digital governance system — China's pandemic Health Code — was inhabited and contested across different social groups. Combining a corpus of over ten thousand scraped public comments with more than twenty in-depth interviews, the study traces how the same infrastructure carried radically divergent social meanings and identity associations from one group to the next.

Its central intervention is to complicate the prevailing account of the "digital divide." Public and policy discourse locates digital exclusion almost entirely in the elderly and the less educated, treating it as a deficit to be remedied through training or simplified interfaces. Fieldwork revealed something more tangled: digitally fluent, highly educated users also developed forms of friction, avoidance, and quiet refusal, while some of those presumed to be "left behind" navigated the system with practised improvisation. Exclusion turned out to be less a property of individuals than a relation produced at the meeting point of infrastructure, institutional pressure, and situated practice.

**Key finding:** Compliance and tacit resistance coexist, and neither maps onto technical literacy. Aggregate adoption figures conceal large-scale surface compliance; genuine acceptance is legible only through qualitative inquiry — a methodological caution as much as an empirical result.

**Methods:** In-depth interviews · Web scraping · Sentiment analysis · Document analysis

### Infrastructure from the Operator's Side: A Checkpoint Ethnography
**Independent Fieldwork · Jul – Sep 2022**

A two-month immersive ethnography conducted while working full-time as a transit security officer, examining human–infrastructure interaction under extreme peak-load conditions. The study documents the cognitive burden and emotional labour borne by frontline operators caught between technological authority, managerial directives, and public pressure.

**Key finding:** Beyond the much-discussed digital divide on the passenger side, frontline staff are a systematically overlooked stakeholder — absorbing equipment friction, ambiguous instructions, and user conflict, and improvising workarounds to keep misaligned top-down protocols functioning at all.

**Methods:** Immersive participant observation · Informal interviews

### Risk Perception and Insurance-Mediated Mobility under Pandemic Conditions
**Shanghai Normal University "Into the Field" Field School · Aug – Sep 2022**

A short comparative study of how insurance reframes travel willingness under pandemic conditions, and how the same policy is assigned different risk perceptions across urban spaces. Drew on digital diaries and opinion mining with fifteen participants across five cities.

**Methods:** Digital diaries · Opinion mining · Comparative analysis
