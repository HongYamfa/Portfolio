Critical Friction Point

The system's "objective" color coding (green/yellow/red) collided with users' subjective risk perception. A 70-year-old user experienced a dignity crash at the scanning moment—forced to choose between compliance and self-respect. The peak emotion was not fear of the virus, but shame of being publicly marked as "non-compliant."

From Field Insight to Design Implication

Field Finding

Users performed "compliant theater" while developing hidden resistance scripts.

→ Design implication: High adoption metrics can mask true rejection. Build "transparency layers" that expose system logic, turning passive compliance into informed consent.

Field Finding

"Not unable to learn—just unwilling to lose dignity."

→ Design implication: For so-called "digitally vulnerable" groups, provide non-digital alternative paths that preserve autonomy, rather than "simplifying" interfaces that still force digital submission.

Field Finding

Frontline staff became the system's "human patch"—absorbing friction the design ignored.

→ Design implication: Service blueprints must include the internal operator's journey, not only the end user. Gray-area workarounds by staff are signals of systemic failure, not individual heroism.

Field Finding

Color semantics were inverted by local emotional logic.

→ Design implication: Visual codes carry cultural payload. In cross-cultural or high-stakes products, semantic mapping must be validated ethnographically—not assumed universal.
