Research Protocol — Methodological Rigor

Independent research does not mean unstructured research. The project followed a formal protocol to ensure ethical safety, analytical validity, and auditability.

🔒

Research Ethics

Informed consent signed by all interviewees. Full anonymization in all outputs. Right to withdraw guaranteed. Audio recordings restricted to researcher-only access.

📊

Mixed Methods

63-item questionnaire for demographic baseline and pain-point prioritization. 30+ semi-structured interviews. Participatory observation in 5+ sites. Social-media scraping (10K+ posts).

🗺️

Field Density

2 cities (Chengdu, Xi'an), 5+ commercial/institutional sites, 6-month longitudinal tracking. Hand-drawn spatial maps for each site to document material layout and power geometry.

From Field Insight to Design Implication

Field Finding

Users performed "compliant theater" while developing hidden resistance scripts.

"Whether the ID is real or fake, what matters is the phone number." — Frontline staff

→ Design implication: High adoption metrics can mask true rejection. Build transparency layers that expose system logic, turning passive compliance into informed consent.

Field Finding

"Not unable to learn—just unwilling to lose dignity."

"Their children taught them not to leak personal information." — Pharmacy manager

→ Design implication: For so-called "digitally vulnerable" groups, resistance is educated caution (anti-fraud family training), not ignorance. Provide non-digital alternative paths that preserve autonomy rather than "simplified" interfaces that still force digital submission.

Field Finding

Frontline staff became the system's "human patch"—absorbing friction the design ignored.

"I will post this on Douyin. No one will support you." — Security guard's gray-area deterrent

→ Design implication: Service blueprints must include the internal operator's journey, not only the end user. Gray-area workarounds by staff are early signals of systemic failure, not individual heroism.

Field Finding

Color semantics were inverted by local emotional logic.

"Compared to yellow, I actually prefer red." — Quarantined respondent

→ Design implication: Visual codes carry cultural payload. In cross-cultural or high-stakes products, semantic mapping must be validated ethnographically—not assumed universal. A "danger" color can become a "certainty" color under local stress.

Field Finding

State-transition opacity created a trust avalanche at the service terminal.

"After turning green, how do you prove you were yellow? No one can tell." — Hospital director

→ Design implication: When a system's state-transition logic (yellow → green) is opaque, the customer-service cost does not fall on the system designer; it is dumped onto the most fragile node in the service chain (private hospitals, small clinics, frontline clerks).

Field Finding

Spatial context within 800 meters produced three divergent interaction ecosystems.

"The superior only checks whether you wrote it, not if it is true." — KTV staff

→ Design implication: A single user journey map is a dangerous fiction. Deploy spatially sensitive service variants that account for local enforcement culture, economic pressure, and physical layout—otherwise frontline workers will invent (and hide) their own.
