Academic Portfolio — Theoretical Grounding

From Animism to Digital Materiality

Two theoretical investigations that reframe how we understand the relationship between humans, objects, and interfaces—foundational to my ethnographic practice.

Connecting Thread

Both projects challenge the Western dualistic split (subject/object, nature/culture, material/immaterial). Whether examining Siberian hunters' mimetic transformations or iPhone users' haptic gestures, the argument is the same: agency is relational, not possessed. This framework directly informs my commercial ethnography— I do not treat users as "rational actors using tools," but as subjects in continuous, embodied negotiation with technologies that shape their personhood.

Project I — Reconfiguring Self-World Relations

Social & Cultural Anthropology Ontological Turn KU Leuven

Beyond the Cogito: Animism, Mimesis, and the Critique of Dualism

A critical engagement with Nurit Bird-David, Rane Willerslev, and Philippe Descola on whether "relatedness" can replace the Cartesian subject as the foundation of human experience.

Core Debate

Bird-David argues that animism is not a cognitive error (Durkheim) but an alternative ontology based on relatedness rather than separation. I test this claim against Willerslev's Yukaghir ethnography and Descola's ontological grid (Animism / Totemism / Analogism / Naturalism).

"The lack of any guaranteed, a priori difference means that difference has to be created constantly through everyday practices." — Willerslev, Soul Hunters
My Position
  • Bird-David's "relatedness" risks dissolving the subject entirely; the cogito cannot be abandoned but must be situated within relational practice.
  • Willerslev's "mimetic hunting" reveals that animists do not fuse with animals; they perform difference through seduction and non-coincidence.
  • Descola's four ontologies are useful heuristics, but his "naturalism" category overstates Western homogeneity and underplays internal dissent.
Implication for Ethnographic Practice

Users are not "segments" to be targeted. Like Yukaghir hunters, they constantly perform and negotiate their difference from the products/brands they interact with. Ethnography must capture this performative tension—how people simultaneously appropriate and resist the identities that technologies offer them.

Project II — Digital Interfaces as Social Agents

Digital Anthropology STS / Materiality KU Leuven

Smartphones between Techniques and Virtuosity: Agency of Cellular Interfaces

An investigation into why "social constructivism" fails to account for digital materials, and how iPhone interfaces produce a new kind of social agency through haptic gesture, fluid motion, and design fetishism.

Core Argument

Drawing on Galloway's "interface effects," Deleuze's dispositif, and Gell's theory of enchantment, I argue that the smartphone is not a neutral tool but an autonomous zone of activity that reshapes personhood through operational sequences (tap, swipe, pinch).

"Interfaces are not things, but processes that effect a result... They bring about transformations in material states." — Galloway, The Interface Effect
Key Concepts Deployed
  • Interface Effects: The screen is not a window but a threshold that mediates between realities, producing specific social and historical outcomes.
  • Fluid Design: Apple's "run like water" philosophy creates an illusion of direct intentionality, masking the algorithmic and material infrastructure beneath.
  • Techniques over Objects: Following Deleuze and Ingold, agency lies not in the device but in the curves of visibility and lines of force it mobilizes.
  • From Fetish to Factish: iPhone marketing transforms the device into a Latourian "factish"—neither pure object nor pure belief, but a hybrid that commands collective attachment.
Implication for Ethnographic Practice

Users do not "use" interfaces; they enter into relational ecologies with them. The tap/swipe/pinch sequence is not merely usability—it is a technique of the body (Mauss) that reshapes habitus. In my fieldwork on health-code systems and AI tools, this framework allows me to see gesture, resistance, and compliance as embodied political practices, not just behavioral data points.

How Theory Grounds Commercial Insight

From Academic Debate to Business Application
  • Descola's ontological pluralism → helps me identify when a product's assumed "naturalism" (objective, universal) clashes with users' "animist" or "analogist" worldviews (e.g., elderly users treating health-code colors as moral judgments rather than data states).
  • Willerslev's mimetic non-coincidence → explains why users perform "compliant theater" while maintaining hidden resistance scripts—difference is constantly performed, not given.
  • Galloway's interface effects → reveals that "frictionless" UX design can actually increase user anxiety by hiding the system's decision logic (e.g., "why did my code turn yellow?").
  • Gell's enchantment / factish → explains brand loyalty and product attachment beyond functional benefit: the iPhone, like the health-code, becomes a social agent that users feel they must appease or negotiate with.
