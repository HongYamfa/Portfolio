Research Context — Why It Matters

Research Question: A Universal System Almost No One Associated with Their Actual Health

The Health Code system achieved near-100% penetration among China's 1.4 billion people within months. From product data, this was a tremendous success. But beneath the surface of mass adoption lay systemic friction, dignitary harm, and invisible evasion behaviours that no quantitative dashboard could capture.

In November 2021, after the first major city (Chengdu) deployed "spatial-temporal proximity" tracking, 82,000 people became "yellow code" overnight. This triggered a chain of embodied conflicts: elderly people chose to forgo medication rather than surrender ID information; security guards threatened resistant young people with posting videos on Douyin; a professor, whose code turned yellow after class, could not board any form of transport and walked hours home through the winter cold.

This study asks: when compliance is mandated rather than voluntary, what does "user adoption" actually mean? How do frontline workers become the system's unofficial patches? What can product teams learn from acts of resistance, rather than from usage metrics alone?

Field insight: Service blueprints drawn at headquarters without spatial elasticity and frontline workaround scripts are destined to fail. The "same" policy produces entirely different user journeys across physical environments, economic pressures, and local enforcement cultures.
Design implication: The system was designed for compliant, rational, independent individuals. In reality it encountered families, hierarchies, bodies, and dignity. Each conflict point reveals a stakeholder the journey map forgot.

From Insight to Strategy

Three field insights translated into actionable product and service design implications. Each finding moves from ethnographic observation to strategic recommendation.

Field Finding 01

Users perform "compliance theatre" while developing hidden resistance scripts: "Not unable to learn — just unwilling to sacrifice dignity"

→ Strategy 1: High usage rates may mask genuine refusal. Build transparency layers that expose system logic, transforming passive compliance into informed consent. Design for "trust verification," not just "usage verification."

→ Strategy 2: The resistance of so-called "digitally vulnerable" groups is educated caution (family anti-fraud education), not ignorance. Offer non-digital alternative pathways that preserve autonomy, rather than a "simplified" interface that forces digital submission.

Field Finding 02

Frontline workers become the system's "human patch" — absorbing friction that design ignored. Colour semantics were overturned by local emotional logic!

"Compared to yellow code, I actually prefer red code." — isolated informant

→ Strategy 1: Service blueprints must include the journey of internal operators, not just end users. Employee workarounds are early signals of systemic failure, not individual heroism. Design escalation pathways rather than exception-handling alone.

→ Strategy 2: Visual codes carry cultural payloads. In cross-cultural or high-stakes products, semantic mappings must be ethnographically validated — not assumed to be universal. A "danger" colour may become a "certainty" colour under local pressure. Always test symbolic interpretation in context.

Field Finding 03

Micro-spatial contexts produce radically different interaction ecologies; ambiguity of state transitions triggers a trust avalanche at service endpoints

"Once you turn green, how do you prove you were ever yellow? Nobody can explain it." — hospital director
"The higher-ups just check whether you wrote something, not whether it's true." — KTV employee

→ Strategy: A single user journey map is a dangerous fiction. Deploy spatially-sensitive service variants that account for local enforcement culture, economic pressure, and physical layout — or frontline staff will invent (and conceal) their own workarounds. Context-aware deployment, not one-size-fits-all rollout.

Methodology — How the Research Was Built

Independent research does not mean unstructured research. The project maintained the flexibility required for ethnographic fieldwork while adhering to formal protocols ensuring ethical safety, analytical validity, and auditability.

🔒

Research Ethics

All informants signed informed consent forms. All outputs fully anonymised. Right to withdraw guaranteed at any time. Recordings accessible only to the researcher.

📊

Mixed Methods

63 surveys for demographic baseline and pain-point ranking. 30+ semi-structured in-depth interviews. 5+ venue participant observations. Social media scraping (10K+ posts) for sentiment and discourse analysis.

🗺️

Field Density

2 cities (Chengdu, Xi'an), 5+ commercial/institutional venues, 6-month longitudinal tracking. Hand-drawn spatial maps of each venue recording material layout and the geometry of power.

🔍

Analytical Framework

Data organised along three analytic layers: digitalisation/datafication, identity/identifier, and personhood — corresponding to the STS and material culture studies' tech-body-person framework.
