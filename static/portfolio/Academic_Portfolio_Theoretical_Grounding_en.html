Co-design: On the Relationship Between Users, Materials, and Digital Systems

Two theoretical perspectives on how anthropological thinking changes the questions a researcher asks — and how that translates into sharper commercial insight.

The Throughline

Conventional user research starts from a simple premise: users have needs, and researchers uncover them. Design anthropology starts somewhere different: users are living inside a world that is constantly changing, and the product is part of that world. This shift in starting point shapes everything — how fieldwork is conducted, how data is interpreted, and how deep the resulting insights go.
Against the Cartesian mind-body split, Tim Ingold's new animism is not the folk belief that objects contain spirits in any traditional sense. It is the recognition that all living beings — human and non-human alike — participate in the world through relations of correspondence. This moves the designer's focus from epistemology (how people think) toward ontology as practice (how they attend, perceive, and act).

Perspective One: The Relationship Between User and Product Is Always Evolving

Design Anthropology Longitudinal Fieldwork

A User Persona Is a Process, Not a Type

We tend to treat user research as a project that ends when we have described what a given type of user is like. But spending enough time in the field reveals that the portrait never stops moving — users are not simply "using" a product, they are developing a relationship with it, and that relationship changes them over time.

Core Perspective

Anthropological tradition reminds us that people and things are not fixed entities; they are processes that shape each other through sustained interaction. Bringing this into user research means shifting attention from "who is this user" to "where is this user's relationship with the product headed" — toward deeper dependency, toward growing friction, or toward uses the designers never anticipated?

In the cross-cultural AI fieldwork project, repeated interviews with the same participants over ten months made this visible: an identical statement about AI carried entirely different meanings in the first interview and the third — relief in one, a kind of self-persuasion in the other. Only longitudinal tracking can tell them apart. The full field analysis is available in the corresponding project report.

Implications for Design Practice

User personas need a temporal dimension, not just a typological description. Rather than asking "what is this type of user like right now," the more valuable question is "how is their relationship with this product evolving?" Tracking these trajectories is what enables genuinely anticipatory design decisions — including identifying oncoming shifts in usage patterns and understanding what role a product plays at different points in a user's life.

Theoretical reference: Tim Ingold's notion of correspondence — people and things are not fixed entities exchanging information but processes that generate each other through sustained, mutually responsive interaction.

Perspective Two: How Interfaces Train Users — and When That Training Breaks Down

Interface Studies Digital Materiality

iPhone as Primary Case: How an Interface Turns Its Own Logic into User "Instinct"

Using the iPhone as a primary case study, this analysis examines how interface design establishes behavioural patterns and emotional attachments in users that go well beyond function. The health code system and subway security system serve as comparative cases, testing the conditions under which this same logic holds — and where it breaks down.

Core Perspective: iPhone Invented a Gesture Culture

When the first iPhone launched, it did something radical: it eliminated every physical button and staked the entire interaction on a glass surface. That decision required Apple to convince users that touching a screen directly with their fingers was "natural" — even though that logic did not previously exist. Tap, drag, swipe, pinch: this vocabulary was invented, not discovered. The core iOS design principle — "run like water," follow your hand, anticipate your intention, respond instantly when you change your mind — was in essence a programme for training a new set of bodily habits and making those habits feel like human instinct.

"Interact with app as it launches… always understand your intention, always responding, always feel you alive." — Apple WWDC18, Designing Fluid Interfaces

Decades later, billions of people navigate digital life through the same gestural vocabulary, and interfaces that violate this logic feel "laggy" or "unnatural" — a feeling that has nothing to do with technical specification. This is the interface effect at its most legible: the product reshaped what users expect their bodies to do, rather than simply responding to what their bodies already did. The brand devotion that surrounds Apple — the anticipation of launch events, the emotional attachment to minimalist form, the identity investment in the ecosystem — is an extension of this same logic: the interface transformed a consumer object into something that carries emotional weight.

Comparative Cases: When Interface Assumptions and User Reality Diverge

China's health code system and urban subway security systems offer an inverse reference point: both attempted to shape user behaviour through interface design, yet encountered something the iPhone never had to face — users with no right to opt out, execution logic driven by administrative mandate rather than experience, and frontline operators who were themselves subject to the system's disciplinary logic. The result was a landscape of workarounds, tacit resistance, and compliance theatre running in parallel. Full ethnographic analyses of both cases are available in the corresponding project reports.

Implications for Design Practice

Whether a product holds together depends on how much overlap exists between the interface's design assumptions and the user's actual situation. The iPhone succeeded because its gestural logic mapped precisely onto the intuitive mechanics of the human hand, and it reinforced that mapping consistently over time. The health code and security systems encountered friction because the user they assumed — a rational, independent individual with a smartphone — diverged systematically from real users in terms of cultural background, dignity, and operational context. Locating the boundary of that overlap is one of the most essential tasks in user research: high adoption figures can mask the very scale of the gap.

Theoretical references: Alexander Galloway on the interface effect; Alfred Gell on technical virtuosity, enchantment, and agency; Paul Dourish on embodied interaction — interfaces produce specific behavioural and emotional consequences through operational sequences and visual language, rather than transmitting information transparently.

How Design Anthropology Translates into Commercial Insight

From Research Perspective to Design Strategy
  • Track how relationships evolve, rather than capturing snapshots. A user's relationship with a product changes over time in ways that a single interview will never reveal. The insights produced by longitudinal tracking and repeated contact reach places that surveys and focus groups cannot.
  • "Frictionless" experience can sometimes destroy meaning. When an interface conceals how it operates — why a health code suddenly turns yellow, for instance — what it severs is not just the user's understanding, but their trust in the system. The trade-off between transparency and fluency needs to be made consciously, not resolved by defaulting to the latter.
  • Deep user attachment is rarely just functional dependency. It is often an entanglement of habit, daily rhythm, and identity. Understanding this is what allows a researcher to judge where a product actually sits in a user's life — and what resistance a design change is likely to produce.
  • Inclusive design is not about patching edge cases. Every user enters a product carrying their own cultural background, physical situation, and life circumstances. Treating this as a basic design assumption rather than an exception to be "accommodated" is what real inclusion looks like.
