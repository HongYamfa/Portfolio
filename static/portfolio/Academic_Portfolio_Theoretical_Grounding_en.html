Co-Design: From New Animism to Embodied Digital Practice

Two theoretical explorations redefining design as a practice of "correspondence" — continuously generated between humans, materials, and digital systems.

Through-Line

Design anthropology is concerned not only with how to design products that satisfy users, but also with how to design with living beings — in a world forever in the process of becoming. Against the Cartesian mind-body split, Tim Ingold's new humanism does not treat life as a property that can be possessed, but sees it as a process of growth unfolding along lines of becoming. His new animism is not the folk belief of "spirits in things" in the traditional sense, but acknowledges that all living beings — human or non-human — participate in the world through relations of "correspondence."

Paul Dourish extends this framework to digital practice: interaction is not the transmission of information, but situated, embodied action. Interfaces are not "used" tools but inhabited environments. Together, Ingold and Dourish provide a framework that makes ethnography and design continuous practices of attending to the world and responding to its affordances.

Embodied Correspondence, Not Conceptual Classification

Design Anthropology New Animism KU Leuven

Beyond Cogito: Correspondence, Meshwork, and the Critique of Dualism

Drawing on Tim Ingold's "new humanism" to respond to Nurit Bird-David's "relational animism" and Philippe Descola's ontological grid — arguing that design must shift from static classification to dynamic correspondence.

Core Debate

Traditional animism research asks: "Do people believe there are souls in objects?" Ingold's new animism inverts the question: "How do humans and non-humans respond to each other in a shared environment?" This shifts anthropology from epistemology (how people think) to ontology-as-practice (how they attend, perceive, and act).

"To dwell is to join in the process of becoming." — Tim Ingold, Being Alive
My Position
  • Ingold rejects the Cartesian "container-self" model. The person is not a closed subject possessing experience; life is an unbounded process of correspondence with an ever-changing world.
  • Bird-David's "relationality" transcends Descola's static grid, but remains at the level of abstract nouns. Ingold's meshwork concept better captures how designer, user, and material interweave in real time.
  • Willerslev's Yukaghir hunters do not "imitate" animals from a distance. They enter correspondence — adjusting posture, rhythm, and attention until the trajectories of human and animal align. This is not representation but co-perception.
  • Descola's four ontologies (naturalism, animism, totemism, analogism) are useful heuristic tools, but their static classificatory structure contradicts the fluid, processual reality that design anthropology must face.
Implications for Design Practice

Users are not target demographics to be aimed at, but living beings with whom products must correspond. "Personas" are not fixed types but temporary nodes in relational meshworks. Design research must therefore trace the process of becoming — how people grow into, around, and sometimes away from the technologies they inhabit.

The Smartphone Between Technological Object and Artwork

Interface Anthropology Digital Materiality KU Leuven

Interface Effects and Cell-Level Agency: How the iPhone Reshapes Social Action

Examining why "social constructionism" cannot explain digital materiality, and how the iPhone interface — through haptic gestures, fluid animation, and design fetishism — produces an entirely new form of social agency.

Core Argument

Drawing on Alexander Galloway's "interface effect," Paul Dourish's "embodied interaction," and Alfred Gell's theory of enchantment, I argue that the smartphone is not a neutral tool but an autonomous zone of activity that reshapes personhood itself through sequences of operation (tap, swipe, pinch).

"The interface is not a thing but a process that produces effects… they induce transformations in material states." — Galloway, The Interface Effect
Key Concepts
  • Interface Effect (Galloway): The screen is not a window but a threshold between different realities, producing specific social and historical consequences. Interfaces are not transparent media; they are themselves a generative force.
  • Fluid Design: Apple's "water-like" design philosophy creates an illusion of direct intentionality, concealing the complexity of the algorithmic and material infrastructure beneath. Users believe they are "directly" touching content, but are in fact interacting with a carefully orchestrated layer of interface effects.
  • Embodied Interaction (Dourish): Agency does not reside in the device itself but in the meanings generated in situated interaction between body and interface. Cognition is not locked in the skull; it is distributed across gesture, screen material, and spatial environment. The value of the iPhone lies not in its hardware but in the sequence of bodily techniques it trains.
  • From Fetish to Factish: iPhone marketing transforms the device into a Latourian "factish" — neither pure object nor pure belief, but a hybrid capable of commanding collective emotional attachment. This is not simple brand loyalty but an ontological binding.
Implications for Ethnographic Practice

Users do not "use" interfaces; they enter into relational ecologies with them. The tap/swipe/pinch sequence is not merely usability — it is bodily technique, reshaping behavioural patterns and psychological structure. In my fieldwork on the health code system and AI tools, this framework allowed me to treat gesture, resistance, and compliance as embodied political practices, rather than behavioural data points to be optimised. When users swipe a screen, they are executing a designed, politically consequential choreography of the body.

How Design Anthropology Translates into Commercial Insight

From Theoretical Debate to Design Strategy
  • Ingold's "correspondence" → Redefines user research: the goal is not to extract needs from passive subjects, but to live with users, learn from them, design from within the meshwork. This yields insights that surveys can never access.
  • Galloway's "interface effect" → Reveals that "frictionless" UX often destroys meaning. When interfaces hide their material logic (e.g.: why has my health code turned yellow?), they sever users' ability to establish correspondence with the system, generating anxiety rather than trust. Commercial design must find a political economy balance between transparency and fluency.
  • Dourish's "embodied design" → Shows that the bond between user and product is often not functional dependency but an entanglement of bodily technique and identity. Understanding how gesture reshapes habitus enables the design of truly inhabitable — rather than merely usable — products.
  • "Co-design" beyond "designing for users" → Repositions the researcher from external observer to co-practitioner. Inclusive design is not about adding features for edge cases; it is acknowledging that every user is the centre of a meshwork, and design must grow with them.
