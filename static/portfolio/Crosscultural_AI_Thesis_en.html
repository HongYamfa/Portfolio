Research Context — Why This Matters

The Problem: When "Making Sense" Requires Rebuilding Your Entire Cognitive Toolkit

By late 2024, generative AI had saturated higher education. Product teams assume AI lowers barriers for all students equally. But for Chinese international students navigating unfamiliar pedagogical cultures — where classroom discussion is graded, oral exams determine pass/fail, and "critical thinking" means something different than in Beijing — AI is not merely a tool. It becomes a cognitive prosthetic, an emotional interlocutor, and an identity threat simultaneously.

The study was born from a personal paradox. As a researcher trained in computer engineering turned anthropologist, I watched my peers — and myself — develop contradictory relationships with ChatGPT. Some treated it as a "brainful yet brainless" companion that liberated productivity. Others saw it eroding the very skills they came abroad to acquire. Many arrived with deep faith in "study for study's sake" — a conviction cultivated through China's exam-oriented system — only to find that faith crumble when confronted with seminar discussions, portfolio assessments, and the expectation to produce guandian (opinions/viewpoints) rather than absorb knowledge.

This research asks: What does "user-centred design" mean when the user is simultaneously adapting to a new culture, a new language, and a new cognitive ecology? How do students modulate heterogeneous resources — visible materials, pre-conscious habitus, and affective capacities — to reorient themselves? And what does the phenomenon of "self-brutalisation" reveal about products that claim to reduce friction but inadvertently amplify anxiety?

User Archetypes — Four Modes of Resourcing

The interlocutors were extremely heterogeneous — no two shared similar backgrounds. Four profiles emerged not from demographics but from how each individual modulated cognitive, material, and affective resources in the face of institutional, semiotic, and affective binds. These are composite portraits anchored in detailed case studies and auto-ethnographic reflection.

Affective Symbiosis
Red — The Intimate Cyborg
Advertising & media undergrad · 9 months agency work · Switched to anthropology · Multiple self-identified disorders (dyslexia, Asperger's, depression, ADHD) · Alternative subculture style (yabi)
"ChatGPT is between you naozi (brainful) and mei naozi (brainless)… it would emancipate the productivity so that I will have more energy to devote myself to other things, such as sleeping… I prefer guandian (opinions)…"
AI as non-judgemental confidant + productivity liberator + emotional validator
Narrative Resistance
Fernand — The Relativist Exile
Documentary director · 7 years at international state-owned TV network · Came to anthropology seeking "universals of human life" · Self-identified ADHD · Relativist, even nihilist tendencies
"I don't like guandian… I am a relativist… I am not saying that I have no guandian, but I am not fond of persuading or being persuaded by anyone… I would not see ChatGPT as a listening partner. Rather, it is just an information processing machine."
Deep aversion to argumentative culture; AI as info-processor, never emotional companion
From Faith to Dwelling
The Researcher — The Correspondent
Computer engineering undergrad · 1 year Chinese anthro MA · 1 year UK dual-degree · Auto-ethnographer · Transitioned from "study as belief" to "dwelling comes first"
"Having studied abroad, your English must be fluent now, right?" — Most of the time, I simply return a polite smile. My English operated largely as a paper-bound skill, far more abstract and disembodied than the inherently visual affordances of Chinese characters.
From Grammarly/DeepL to ChatGPT; from knowledge acquisition to dialogic correspondence
Inertial Brutalisation
Childe — The Silenced Strategist
Hengshui High School alumnus · Completed humanities BA and MA in China · Pursuing philosophy · Self-diagnosed ADHD · High exam inertia
"Because my ability to organise language on the spot isn't good, I might prepare by writing down questions or things I want to say while reading the materials beforehand… But even when I've read all the materials and even prepared my comments in advance, I often still end up saying very little."
Prepared competence collapses in real-time oral performance; seminar silence as structural bind

Critical Findings — Emotional Arc & Core Discoveries

Affective Trajectory: From Honeymoon to Multiple Bind
Arrival
Excited, overwhelmed, "everything is possible"
First Semester
Confused, self-blame, "why can't I keep up?"
Mid-Programme
Numb, cynical, "this is just how it is"
Second Year
Strategic, selective, "I take what I need"
Resolution
Ambivalent integration, unresolved grief
Key insight: The emotional curve does not follow a simple "culture shock" U-shape. It plateaus in what Xiang Biao calls "self-brutalisation" — students impose ever-higher demands on themselves to compensate for perceived deficits, while institutional uncertainty and scarcity of recognition feed a recursive circuit of moralised diligence. The bind is not merely cultural; it is multiple — institutional-evaluative, semiotic-algorithmic, and affective-ethical all at once.

Six core findings extracted from longitudinal interview data, classroom observations, and digital behaviour tracking. Each finding connects ethnographic observation to product strategy.

Field Finding 01
01

AI as "Brainful yet Brainless": A Double-Edged Symbiosis

"ChatGPT is between you naozi and mei naozi… it would emancipate the productivity… everyone is using AI and you don't use it and then you go on and lose some of your abilities." — Red

→ Strategy: For neurodivergent users, AI functions simultaneously as cognitive scaffold and ability atrophier. Products should design for "graduated dependence" — actively rebuilding the skills they temporarily substitute for, rather than optimising solely for convenience. Track not just task completion but skill transfer.

Field Finding 02
02

The Guandian Aversion: When "Opinion" Culture Meets Narrative Tradition

"I don't like guandian… I always wrote narrative essays in high school Chinese exams. I've always resisted argumentative essays that require stating and supporting my guandian." — Fernand

→ Strategy: EdTech platforms built on Western pedagogical assumptions — discussion forums, debate formats, argumentative essays — systematically disadvantage users from narrative-priority cultures. Multimodal submission options (narrative, visual, dialogic) are not accessibility luxuries; they are equity requirements for cross-cultural products.

Field Finding 03
03

Self-Brutalisation as a Product Design Failure

"Many Chinese students are proud to be here but get crushed by exam fears, not engaging with ideas." — Leo

→ Strategy: When students experience "self-brutalisation" — imposing punitive demands on themselves — the problem is not individual psychology but institutional opacity. Products that hide assessment criteria, expect implicit participation norms, or assume prior cultural knowledge create anxiety loops. Proactive transparency about expectations and alternative pathways beats reactive wellbeing support.

Field Finding 04
04

The Parallel-Classroom Effect: Literacy vs. Oracy as Interface War

"Every time we've had a group discussion, it feels like we're two parallel worlds separated by glass." — Social media post by Chinese international student

→ Strategy: Chinese students trained in exam-oriented literacy often possess deep reading competence but embodied oracy gaps in real-time performance. Collaboration tools cannot assume shared norms for turn-taking, disagreement, or silence. Build culture-setting onboarding that makes implicit discussion protocols explicit — not just language tools.

Field Finding 05
05

Emotional Value as the Real AI Value Proposition

"It will acknowledge your emotions… no matter what opinion you express, it will acknowledge it. Even if it doesn't agree, it will first acknowledge it and then start to speak." — Red on ChatGPT's emotional support

→ Strategy: The killer feature for cross-cultural, neurodivergent, or anxious users is not information accuracy but interpretive generosity — the ability to validate half-formed thoughts without judgement. Current AI products optimise for correctness; they should optimise for recognition and emotional safety. An "acknowledge first, correct second" interaction mode would outperform raw accuracy for this user group.

Field Finding 06
06

From Study to Dwelling: The Need for Products That Support "Correspondence"

"What I learned that year was not primarily information, knowledge, or language, but an attitude toward life: the capacity to face both others and myself independently." — Researcher's auto-ethnographic reflection

→ Strategy: Products designed for "international students" typically target the honeymoon phase — visa, housing, course selection. They miss the long-tail crisis of existential reorientation. Drawing on Tim Ingold's "correspondence anthropology," future products should support ongoing wayfaring — not just information delivery but the slow, iterative process of learning to dwell in a new cognitive and cultural ecology.

Methodology — How the Study Was Built

The research was framed as an ethnographic experiment of encounter, drawing on Gregory Bateson's ecology of mind and Tim Ingold's correspondence anthropology. Methodological rigour was maintained through multi-scalar, digital, and dialogical approaches.

🪞

Auto-Ethnography

The researcher was simultaneously insider (Chinese international student, computer engineering-to-anthropology transition) and outsider (fieldworker), using self-observation to calibrate interview questions across two years of personal transformation.

📱

Digital Ethnography

Online interaction analysis and digital behaviour tracking — capturing how students interacted with AI agents, learning platforms, and social media, including usage frequency, communication modes, and shifts over time.

🎭

Dialogical Ethnography

Following Crapanzano and Tedlock, the study prioritised shadow dialogues and unsaid elements over surface statements. Original conversations are presented to evoke "visceral" understanding rather than persuade through abstraction.

🔄

Multi-Scalar Design

Single university site, multiple analytical scales — from visible materials (sociological) to pre-conscious habitus (structural) to affective capacities (ethnological) — following Bateson's three-level framework from Naven.
