Research Context — Why It Matters

Research Question: When "Understanding" Requires Rebuilding the Entire Cognitive Toolkit

By late 2024, generative AI had penetrated higher education on a massive scale. Product teams assumed AI lowered the threshold equally for all students. But for Chinese international students adapting to an unfamiliar pedagogical culture — whether seminar participation counts for grades, whether exams determine failure, what "critical thinking" actually means here — AI is not merely a tool. It is simultaneously a cognitive prosthetic, an emotional interlocutor, and an identity threat.

As a researcher who transitioned from computer engineering to anthropology, I witnessed my peers — and myself — developing ambivalent relationships with ChatGPT. Some treated it as a partner who was "sometimes smart, sometimes not," liberating their productivity. Others felt it was eroding the capacities they had come abroad to develop. Many arrived with deep beliefs in "learning for its own sake," only to find those beliefs fracturing in seminars demanding live exchange rather than knowledge absorption.

This study asks: when users are simultaneously adapting to a new culture, a new language, and a new cognitive ecology, what does "user-centred design" mean? How do students orchestrate heterogeneous resources — visible materials, pre-conscious habitus, affective capacities — to reorient themselves? And what does the phenomenon of "self-brutalisation" reveal about products that claim to reduce friction but inadvertently amplify anxiety?

User Archetypes — Intersecting Resource Orchestration Patterns

Informants were highly heterogeneous — no two had similar backgrounds. The archetypes below do not emerge from demographic segmentation, but from how each person orchestrated cognitive, material, and affective resources under multiple institutional, symbolic, and emotional constraints.

Affective Symbiosis
Red — The Intimate Cyborg
BA in Advertising & Media · 9 months at an ad agency · Switched to Anthropology · Multiple neurodivergences (dyslexia, Asperger's, depression, ADHD)
"ChatGPT is somewhere between having a brain and not — but it liberates productivity, so I can invest more energy elsewhere... I prefer to express and hear viewpoints through conversation..."
AI as non-judgmental confidant + productivity liberator + emotional validator
Narrative Resistance
Fernand — The Relativist Exile
Documentary director · 7 years at international state TV networks · Came to Anthropology seeking "universals of human life" · Self-diagnosed ADHD · Relativist, even nihilist tendencies
"I don't like viewpoints... I'm a relativist... I'm not saying I have no opinions, but I don't like persuading or being persuaded... I wouldn't use ChatGPT as a listening partner — it's just an information processing machine."
Deep aversion to debate culture; AI as information processor only, never emotional companion
Inertial Brutalisation
Childe — The Silent Strategist
Graduated from Hengshui High School · Completed BA and MA in humanities in China · Pursuing philosophy · Self-diagnosed ADHD · High exam-preparation inertia
"Because my ability to organise language on the spot is poor, I might write down what I want to ask in advance... but even after reading all the materials and preparing comments, I often still can't say anything."
Prepared competence collapses in real-time oral performance; seminar silence is a structural constraint

Core Findings — Affective Arc and Key Discoveries

Affective Trajectory: From Honeymoon to Multiple Constraints
Arrival
Excitement, overwhelmed, "anything is possible"
Semester 1
Confused, self-blaming, "Why can't I keep up?"
Mid-Programme
Numb, cynical, "This is just how it is"
Year Two
Strategic, selective, "I take only what I need"
Adaptation
Ambivalent integration, unresolved regret
Key insight: The affective curve does not follow the simple U-shape of "culture shock." It stalls in what Xiang Biao calls self-brutalisation — students imposing ever-higher demands on themselves to compensate for perceived deficits, while institutional uncertainty and scarcity of recognition feed a recursive loop of moralised diligence. This constraint is not merely cultural; it is multiple — institutional-evaluative, symbolic-algorithmic, and affective-ethical simultaneously.

From Insight to Strategy

Six field insights translated into actionable product and service design implications.

Field Finding 01

Double-Edged Symbiosis: "Sometimes smart, sometimes not" — claims no emotional function yet provides emotional value

"Whatever you express, it acknowledges. Even if it disagrees, it acknowledges first... Everyone uses AI; not using it is like lacking a certain ability." — Red

→ Strategy: For neurodivergent users, AI is simultaneously a cognitive scaffold and a capacity atrophier. Products should design for "graduated dependency" — rebuilding displaced skills even as it substitutes for them. Track skill-transfer metrics, not just task completion rates.

Field Finding 02

Meaning Conflicts Across Educational Expectations: Narrative/Written vs. Argumentative/Spoken

"Every seminar felt like two parallel worlds separated by glass." — Social media post

→ Strategy 1: EdTech built on Western pedagogical assumptions systematically disadvantages users from narrative-priority cultures. Multi-modal submission (narrative, visual, dialogic) is an equity necessity, not an accessibility luxury.

→ Strategy 2: Deep reading competence and real-time oral performance coexist with an embodied gap. Build cultural setting guides that make implicit norms — turn-taking, expressing disagreement, the etiquette of silence — explicit.

Field Finding 03

Self-Brutalisation as Institutional Design Failure

"Chinese students are proud to be here, yet crushed by exam anxiety." — Leo

→ Strategy 1: "Self-brutalisation" is not an individual psychological problem but a product of institutional opacity. Proactive transparency about expectations and alternative pathways outperforms passive mental-health remediation.

→ Strategy 2: Products designed for international students often only target the honeymoon phase, missing the crisis of existential re-orientation. Support dwelling — the slow process of learning to inhabit a new cognitive ecology — rather than just information transfer.

Methodology — How the Research Was Built

The research was framed as an ethnography of encounter, drawing on Gregory Bateson's ecology of mind and Tim Ingold's anthropology of correspondence. Methodological rigour was maintained through multi-scale, digital, and dialogic methods.

🪞

Auto-ethnography

The researcher is simultaneously insider (Chinese international student, CS-to-anthropology) and outsider (fieldworker), calibrating interview questions through self-observation during a two-year personal transition.

📱

Digital Ethnography

Analysis of online interactions and digital behavioural traces — capturing students' interactions with AI agents, learning platforms, and social media, including frequency of use, communication patterns, and changes over time.

🎭

Dialogic Ethnography

The research prioritises shadow conversations and the unspoken over surface statements. Raw dialogue is presented to evoke embodied understanding rather than persuade through abstraction.

🔄

Multi-Scale Design

Although situated within a single university, the study employs multiple analytic scales — from visible materials (sociological) to pre-conscious habitus (structural) to affective capacities (ethnological) — following Bateson's three-layer framework from Naven.
