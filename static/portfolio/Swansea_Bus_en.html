Research Background

Research Question: When "More Passengers" Masks a Deeper Service Crisis

The Swansea Council's "Free Ride" scheme returned for the 2022 Christmas season, driving an immediate surge in passenger numbers. Yet beneath these stellar figures, user trust was already severely eroded: real-time tracking displayed "Due," but the buses never arrived; passengers were turned away due to overcapacity; and elderly users with low digital literacy were effectively locked out of the system.

The study was triggered by a specific incident: on October 5th, a storm knocked down a large tree near the Fulton House roundabout, forcing a temporary relocation of the bus stop. Two months later, the stop had been restored to its original location, but without any official notification. An elderly university employee remains uncertain to this day whether they are standing in the correct spot. The information gap between official channels and frontline reality was never bridged.

This study asks: When metrics (ridership) and experience (reliability) decouple, what does "user satisfaction" actually mean? How does the failure of information architecture—contradictory notices, ambiguous app design, and missing real-time updates—cumulatively transform into frustration across different user groups? And what happens when the glowing numbers brought by free incentives mask systemic service decline?

Core Findings

Within a 2 km radius, three field sites revealed three distinct failure modes. The "same" bus service produced entirely different user journeys due to disparities in stop infrastructure, app reliability, and passenger resilience.

Confusion

Fulton House Roundabout

Failure Mode
Physical information failure — more than three contradictory notices left un-updated two months after the storm
User Impact
Elderly staff remain unsure of where to wait; passengers waited at the wrong location for over thirty minutes
"We waited here for over half an hour, watching one bus after another leave. When we finally became the only two people left on the platform, a staff member came over to ask which bus we were taking, and then pointed to the other side."
Ghost Bus

Christina Street (City Centre)

Failure Mode
Algorithmic unreliability — real-time tracking displays "Due," but the bus never appears
User Impact
Two young passengers chased a "ghost bus" and waited for 25 minutes; one walked home, the other was stranded in the strong wind
"The originally scheduled bus turned into a 'ghost'. One passenger left first — fortunately, he lived right next door. Three minutes later, the other realized something was wrong too, but had nowhere to go and could only wait in the strong wind."
Abandoned

Bernard Street (Peripheral Stops)

Failure Mode
Service inequality — core routes are prioritized while peripheral areas are marginalized due to "low profitability"
User Impact
"Peripheral" areas are excluded from the free incentives; residents spontaneously form community groups to document this systemic neglect
"Great, but Pennard isn't included in the summer because we are seen as peripheral." — Andrea Brooks
Field Insight: The same transportation network manifests three distinct failure modes within a 2 km radius — information failure (physical layer), algorithmic "ghosting" (digital layer), and spatial abandonment (structural layer). A product team that only looks at aggregate ridership would miss all three failures.

From Insight to Strategy

Three field findings translated into actionable product and service design implications.

Finding 01

Two Modes of Information Failure: Contradictory Physical Notices and Digital "Ghost Buses"

"There are more than three notices related to route information at this stop. Are they complementary? Which one is the latest? Two of them even contradict each other." — Fulton House Passenger; "Both were staring intently at their phones displaying tracking info. But the scheduled bus became a 'ghost', and one of them left." — Christina Street Field Observation

→ Strategy: Information failures occur simultaneously across physical and digital layers but are often handled separately. The physical layer requires a single source of truth — stacking multiple contradictory notices only creates a wall of text that bewilders users. The digital layer must be designed for "how to communicate uncertainty" — a real-time tracker displaying "Due" when no bus is coming destroys trust far more than having no tracker at all. An honest "We are uncertain" status beats false precision.

Finding 02

Flattering Ridership Numbers Mask Systemic Service Decline

"More passengers during the free ride period does not equal better service." — Core Finding Headline; "I rarely take the bus now, especially when it's free because it's always packed, which makes me incredibly anxious." — Chelsea Barrett

→ Strategy: Surges in ridership during promotional periods are dangerous "vanity metrics." Product teams should track retention cohorts — do first-time users return after the discount ends? Are their experience ratings positive? A report lacking satisfaction and retention data, showing only aggregated total passenger volume, presents a dangerously distorted picture.

Finding 03

Users from Diverse Cultural Backgrounds Approach the Same Interface with Different Mental Models

"After missing it multiple times, I realized that the bolded times were unreliable, unlike Google Maps' 'scheduled time'." — Zheyu's discovery on the First Bus App

→ Strategy: Users from different transit cultures interpret the same interface elements differently. Bold text meant "real-time" to Zheyu (the convention in Chinese apps), but actually represented "scheduled time" in the First Bus App. Icons and typography must undergo cross-cultural validation — never assume a visual symbol will be interpreted the same way by everyone.

Methodology

This rapid study combined five methods over four weeks, demonstrating how mixed-methods research produces actionable insights under tight time constraints — a skillset that transfers directly to agile product teams.

👁️

Participant Observation

Conducted direct observation at 5+ bus stops over two weekends, spanning from the Singleton campus to the city center and peripheral residential areas. Field notes documented passenger behavior, wait times, boarding confusion, and interactions between passengers, physical infrastructure, and mobile apps.

🎤

In-depth Interviews

Completed 10+ semi-structured interviews with participants from 5+ countries (Malaysia, Kenya, China, UK, Nigeria), ranging in age from their 20s to 60s. Interview guides covered first-time experiences, app usage habits, alternative transit choices, and the process of trust-building.

📱

App Heuristic Evaluation

Performed systematic walkthroughs of the First Bus mobile application to identify interface inconsistencies (ambiguity between bold and regular typography, unclear icon meanings, dead-end search paths) and contrasted findings with Google Maps on identical routes.

💬

Social Media Analysis

Analyzed over 200 comments from the Swansea Online Facebook page and community groups like "Bus Service Godre'r Graig & Ystalyfera." Coded comments for sentiment and conducted thematic analysis on complaint typologies, First Bus response patterns, and community self-organization strategies.
